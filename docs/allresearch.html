Building Sustainable Research Practices

Sustainable research goes beyond scientific excellence by integrating environmental awareness, social responsibility, and transparency into all stages of the research processâ€”from planning and data collection to analysis, dissemination, and reuse.

By embedding sustainability into research practices, we promote the efficient use of resources, fair and inclusive collaboration, and the production of knowledge that can be trusted, reused, and built upon.
Good Sustainable Practice supports robust methodologies, responsible data management, and reflexive research cultures that consider the broader impacts of scientific work.
In doing so, research becomes a key contributor to sustainable development, innovation, and informed decision-making.

Literature Research

Sustainability considerations:

A thorough literature search helps reduce unnecessary repetition of experiments, saving resources, time, and funding. Understanding existing data can also open opportunities for collaboration and data reuse â€” both of which minimize environmental impact by reducing redundant lab work and travel. Sharing well-documented reviews and curated resources further contributes to collective scientific efficiency and transparency.

Questions you could ask:

  • Does my research question address a true knowledge gap, or could I build on existing work instead of repeating it?
  • Are there available datasets, samples, or protocols that I could reuse or adapt?
  • Have similar studies already been performed at my institution or within existing collaborations?
  • Can I connect with other groups to share findings or combine efforts before starting new experiments?

Research Question

Sustainability considerations:

A clear, well-defined research question guides efficient experimental design and minimizes unnecessary work, materials, and data collection. Beyond scientific relevance, formulating socially and environmentally responsible questions ensures that research contributes positively to both knowledge and society.

Considering the broader impact at this early stage helps align your project with principles of open, fair, and sustainable science.

Questions you could ask:

  • Does my research address a problem that has meaningful societal or environmental relevance?
  • Could the outcomes of my project contribute to more sustainable practices, policies, or technologies?
  • Can I design my study to generate insights that are reusable or that reduce future experimental burden?
  • Am I engaging stakeholders (e.g., clinicians, communities, or industry) to ensure the research question meets real-world needs?

Mentorship

Sustainability considerations:

Strong mentorship is a cornerstone of sustainable research culture. Clear communication, structured supervision, and accessible guidance prevent avoidable mistakes, wasted materials, and unnecessary repetition â€” all of which save time, energy, and resources in the long run. A supportive mentorship environment also promotes well-being, collaboration, and knowledge sharing, helping early-career researchers grow into responsible and efficient scientists. Sustainability in mentorship means building systems that value learning, transparency, and continuous feedback as much as productivity.

Questions you could ask:

  • Who is formally responsible for supervising and supporting my project?
  • Do I clearly understand the purpose and design of my experiments or analyses?
  • Have I received enough feedback to perform my work efficiently and reproducibly?
  • When is my next meeting with my supervisor or mentor â€” and am I well prepared for it?
  • Are there opportunities for peer mentoring or lab-wide discussions to share expertise and reduce duplicated efforts?
  • How can I communicate challenges early to avoid unnecessary setbacks or wasted work?
